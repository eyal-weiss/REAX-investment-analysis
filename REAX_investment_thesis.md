# The Real Brokerage Inc. (NASDAQ: REAX) — Investment Thesis

*March 2026 | Based on FY2025 audited financials and bottom-up financial model*

---

## Executive Summary

The Real Brokerage is a technology-first residential real estate brokerage that has grown from $122M in revenue (2021) to nearly $2.0B (2025) by recruiting agents onto a cloud-based platform with a compelling value proposition: higher commission splits, equity participation, and integrated financial services. The stock currently trades at a significant discount to intrinsic value across all modeled scenarios, driven by the market's tendency to evaluate REAX on GAAP metrics that are distorted by heavy stock-based compensation — a transitory expense that masks the company's genuine cash-generating ability.

Our bottom-up model, anchored in agent-count growth, per-agent productivity, and a per-segment financial services build-out, yields the following DCF-implied share prices (diluted):

| Scenario | Implied Price | 2030E Revenue | 2030E Adj EBITDA | Rev CAGR (25–30) |
|----------|--------------|--------------|-----------------|-----------------|
| **Bull** | **$23.20** | $7.2B | $862M | 29.4% |
| **Base** | **$14.00** | $5.2B | $498M | 21.6% |
| **Bear** | **$7.02** | $3.5B | $221M | 12.3% |

The thesis rests on three pillars: (1) continued agent recruitment into a platform with structural advantages, (2) an emerging high-margin financial services business that fundamentally reshapes the margin profile, and (3) a debt-free balance sheet generating substantial free cash flow that funds share buybacks and organic investment.

## Company Overview

**What REAX does.** The Real Brokerage operates as the brokerage-of-record for independent real estate agents across North America. When an agent affiliated with REAX closes a home sale, REAX earns a "take rate" (commission split) on the transaction's gross commission. In 2025, that take rate was approximately 2.6% of GMV, producing $1.97B in revenue on $75.3B in gross merchandise value.

**Why agents join.** REAX offers agents 85/15 commission splits (85% to agent, 15% to brokerage) with a $12K annual cap — after which the agent keeps 100%. This is materially better than traditional brokerages (often 70/30 with no cap). Additionally, agents receive REAX stock grants for production milestones and recruiting, creating network-effect dynamics.

**Scale as of FY2025:**

| Metric | FY2025 |
|--------|--------|
| Agents | 31,739 |
| Transactions | 185,314 |
| GMV | $75.3B |
| Revenue | $1.97B |
| Adj. EBITDA | $62.9M |

| Free Cash Flow | $62.1M |

## Key Concepts an Investor Must Understand

### 1. GAAP Net Income Is Misleading — Follow the Cash

REAX reported a GAAP net loss of $8.1M in 2025. Yet it generated $62.9M in Adjusted EBITDA and $62.1M in free cash flow — enough to fund $39.4M in share buybacks while growing cash on the balance sheet.

The disconnect is almost entirely explained by **stock-based compensation (SBC)**, which was $68.1M in 2025 (3.5% of revenue). SBC is a real economic cost — it dilutes shareholders — but it is a *non-cash* expense. For a high-growth company using equity to attract agents and retain employees, GAAP net income dramatically understates true earning power. As growth moderates and SBC declines as a percentage of revenue (modeled at 3.3% → 2.4% by 2030), GAAP profitability will converge toward cash profitability.

### 2. Gross Margin Looks Low — But That's the Business Model

REAX's blended gross margin was 8.4% in 2025. That sounds thin, but it's structural to real estate brokerage — the 91.6% "COGS" is primarily commissions paid to the agents themselves. The relevant comparison is not to software companies but to other brokerages:

- COMP (Compass): similar margin profile
- EXPI (eXp Realty): similar margin profile
- Traditional brokerages: often lower margins due to physical office costs

The key insight is that REAX's *operating leverage* is exceptional. On $704M of incremental revenue in 2025 (vs. 2024), the company expanded Adjusted EBITDA by $22.9M. The fixed-cost base (technology platform, corporate staff) grows much slower than revenue, so each incremental dollar of revenue drops through at a higher rate.

### 3. Adjusted EBITDA vs. GAAP Operating Income

Our model emphasizes **Adjusted EBITDA** — defined as gross profit minus cash revenue share minus cash operating expenses (excluding SBC and D&A). This metric isolates the recurring cash-generating capacity of the business. It strips out:

- **SBC** ($68M in 2025): Non-cash, declining as % of revenue
- **D&A** ($1.9M in 2025): Minimal given asset-light model
- **One-time settlements** ($0.75M in 2025): Non-recurring legal costs

GAAP operating income includes all of these and was -$2.3M in 2025 for the brokerage segment alone. The gap between GAAP Op Income and Adj EBITDA narrows as SBC moderates — by 2030 Base case, GAAP Operating Income reaches $371M vs. Adj EBITDA of $498M.

### 4. Revenue Share Is an Operating Expense, Not COGS

REAX pays agents a "revenue share" — a bonus tied to the production of agents they've recruited. In 2025, this was $60.5M (3.1% of revenue). Revenue share is classified as an operating expense, not cost of revenue. This is important because it means **gross margin** doesn't capture the full cost of agent compensation. When assessing total agent economics, one should look at COGS + revenue share combined.

Revenue share is the engine of REAX's agent recruitment flywheel. Each agent has an incentive to recruit others, creating viral growth without proportional marketing spend.

### 5. The Balance Sheet Is a Fortress

REAX is **debt-free**. As of FY2025:

| Asset | $K |
|-------|-----|
| Cash & equivalents | $33,213 |
| Short-term investments | $16,731 |
| Restricted cash | $26,338 |
| **Total liquid assets** | **$76,282** |
| Total debt | $0 |

This means WACC = Cost of Equity (no debt weighting needed), and the company has maximum financial flexibility. The net cash position ($49.9M unrestricted) is additive to equity value in the DCF.

### 6. Agent Growth Is the Master Variable

Nearly every line in the model flows from agent count. More agents → more transactions → more GMV → more brokerage revenue. And crucially, more agents → larger captive audience for financial services cross-sell. Historical agent growth has been exceptional:

| Year | Agents (EOY) | YoY Growth |
|------|-------------|-----------|
| 2021 | 3,850 | — |
| 2022 | 8,200 | +113% |
| 2023 | 13,650 | +66% |
| 2024 | 24,140 | +77% |
| 2025 | 31,739 | +31% |

Growth is decelerating (from triple-digit to ~31%), which is natural as the base grows. Our model assumes continued deceleration: 22% (Base 2026) → 11% (Base 2030). Even the Bear case assumes 15% → 6%, reflecting the reality that REAX is still a small player in a fragmented market with ~1.5M licensed agents in the US.

### 7. Take Rate and ASP — The Revenue Formula

Revenue = Agents x Transactions/Agent x Average Sale Price x Take Rate.

Each component matters:

- **Transactions/agent** (~5.8 in 2025): Reflects agent productivity and market conditions. Modestly growing in projections (5.9 → 6.1 in Base) as the agent mix matures.
- **Average sale price** (~$406K in 2025): Driven by housing market conditions. We assume 2.0–2.5% annual ASP growth (roughly inflation).
- **Take rate** (~2.6%): REAX's effective commission on GMV. Held roughly flat in projections. This is a function of the agent split structure and is relatively stable.

### 8. NOL Carryforwards Shelter Near-Term Taxes

REAX has accumulated net operating losses from its growth phase:

| Jurisdiction | NOL ($K) |
|-------------|---------|
| US Federal | $44,680 |
| US State | $39,467 |
| Canada Federal | $12,276 |
| Israel | $4,048 |

These shelter taxable income until exhausted. In the Base case, the NOLs shelter income through 2027, with cash taxes beginning in 2028 as the 27% Canadian statutory rate applies. This means near-term FCF is higher than steady-state due to the tax shield.

## The Financial Services Opportunity

This is the most underappreciated element of the REAX story. The company has launched three ancillary financial services — **One Real Title**, **One Real Mortgage**, and **Wallet (reZEN)** — that sit alongside the core brokerage and monetize the same transaction flow at dramatically higher margins.

### 2025 Segment Economics

| Segment | Revenue | Gross Margin | Operating Income |
|---------|---------|-------------|-----------------|
| NA Brokerage | $1,956M | 8.1% | -$2.3M |
| One Real Title | $5.0M | 82.3% | -$4.1M |
| One Real Mortgage | $6.0M | 47.7% | -$2.2M |
| Wallet (reZEN) | $0.9M | 77.1% | -$0.7M |

All three FS segments are currently operating at a loss because fixed OpEx (staff, licensing, technology buildout) exceeds their still-small gross profit. But the margin structures are extraordinary: Title at 82% gross margin vs. Brokerage at 8%. Every dollar of Title revenue that displaces a dollar of Brokerage revenue in the mix adds ~74 cents of incremental gross profit.

### The Attach Rate Framework

Our model projects FS growth via **attach rates** — the percentage of brokerage transactions that also use each financial service. This is the right framework because:

1. The transaction flow already exists (185K transactions in 2025)
2. Growth comes from *adoption within the existing agent base*, not just new agent additions
3. The denominator (total transactions) grows naturally with agent growth

**Base case attach rate trajectory:**

| Segment | 2025 (est.) | 2026E | 2028E | 2030E | Rev/Attached Txn |
|---------|------------|-------|-------|-------|-----------------|
| Title | ~0.3% | 2% | 4.5% | 8% | ~$10K |
| Mortgage | ~0.8% | 1% | 3% | 6% | ~$4K |
| Wallet | ~2.4% | 2% | 7% | 15% | ~$200 |

### Impact on Blended Margin

As FS scales, the blended gross margin improves significantly. In the Base case:

| Year | Brokerage GM | Blended GM | FS Uplift | FS % of Revenue |
|------|-------------|-----------|-----------|----------------|
| 2025A | 8.4% | 8.4% | — | 0.6% |
| 2026E | 8.8% | 10.3% | +1.5pp | 2.2% |
| 2028E | 9.5% | 12.8% | +3.3pp | 5.1% |
| 2030E | 10.0% | 15.8% | +5.8pp | 9.0% |

By 2030 in the Base case, financial services contribute ~9% of revenue but drive a nearly 6 percentage point improvement in blended gross margin. This is a meaningful margin transformation driven by the mix shift toward high-margin segments.

### Sensitivity: FS Adoption Speed vs. Profitability

The pace of FS adoption is inherently uncertain. Our sensitivity analysis shows the portfolio effect:

| FS Attach Multiplier | FS % of Revenue (5yr avg) | Blended GM (5yr avg) | GM vs. Base |
|---------------------|--------------------------|---------------------|------------|
| 0.25x (Very slow) | 1.6% | 10.6% | -2.9pp |
| 0.50x (Slow) | 3.1% | 11.6% | -1.9pp |
| 1.00x (Base) | 6.0% | 13.4% | — |
| 1.50x (Fast) | 8.7% | 15.2% | +1.8pp |
| 2.00x (Very fast) | 11.2% | 16.9% | +3.4pp |

*Note: This table shows 5-year averages (2026–2030) across the projection period. Individual year values increase over time — for instance, at 1.00x the FS share of revenue rises from 2.2% in 2026 to 9.0% in 2030, averaging 6.0%.*

Even at half the base-case adoption speed, FS lifts blended gross margin above brokerage-only levels. At twice the adoption speed, blended GM reaches nearly 17% — a fundamentally different margin profile. The optionality is asymmetric: the downside still improves margins vs. brokerage alone, while the upside reshapes the business economics.

## DCF Valuation Methodology

### Approach

We use a 5-year Discounted Cash Flow with a Gordon Growth terminal value. Key parameters:

| Parameter | Value | Rationale |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| WACC | 12.0% | CAPM: 4.3% risk-free + 1.40 beta x 5.5% ERP. No debt adjustment needed. |
| Terminal Growth | 3.0% | Reflects long-term nominal GDP growth; real estate correlates with housing formation + inflation |
| FCF Definition | Adj EBITDA - Cash Taxes - Capex | Excludes SBC (non-cash), includes NOL tax shield |

## Exit Multiple Valuation (2030E)

The DCF above relies on a Gordon Growth terminal value, which is sensitive to perpetuity assumptions. As a cross-check, we value REAX using **exit multiples** applied to 2030E financials and discounted back to present value at 12% WACC. This approach asks: "If an investor bought the stock today and sold in 2030 at a given multiple, what would the annualized return imply as a fair price?"

**2030E financials by scenario:**

| Metric | Bull | Base | Bear |
|--------|------|------|------|
| Adj EBITDA | $862M | $498M | $221M |
| Net Income (GAAP) | $515M | $272M | $94M |

*Discount factor: 5 years at 12% WACC = 1.76x. All "PV Today" prices reflect the 2030 exit value discounted to present.*

### 1. EV/EBITDA Exit Multiple

Enterprise value = 2030E Adj EBITDA x multiple, plus $50M net cash, divided by 239M diluted shares. Peer context: mature brokerages trade at 6–10x EV/EBITDA; high-growth tech-enabled platforms at 10–15x.

**Implied share price (present value) by scenario and exit multiple:**

| EV/EBITDA | Bull | Base | Bear |
|-----------|------|------|------|
| **6x** | $12.42 | $7.22 | $3.28 |
| **8x** | $16.51 | $9.58 | $4.33 |
| **10x** | $20.61 | $11.95 | $5.38 |
| **12x** | $24.71 | $14.31 | $6.43 |
| **15x** | $30.86 | $17.86 | $8.01 |

At 10x EV/EBITDA — a reasonable multiple for a profitable, growing brokerage with a scaling FS business — the Base case implies $11.95 today. At 12x, it reaches $14.31, closely corroborating the DCF-derived $14.00.

### 2. P/E Exit Multiple

Equity value = 2030E Net Income x multiple, divided by 239M diluted shares. Note that GAAP net income includes SBC expense ($126M Base, $157M Bull, $99M Bear in 2030), so P/E multiples here are on *fully-loaded* GAAP earnings — a conservative basis.

**Implied share price (present value) by scenario and exit multiple:**

| P/E | Bull | Base | Bear |
|-----|------|------|------|
| **15x** | $18.36 | $9.70 | $3.34 |
| **20x** | $24.48 | $12.93 | $4.45 |
| **25x** | $30.59 | $16.16 | $5.56 |
| **30x** | $36.71 | $19.39 | $6.68 |
| **35x** | $42.83 | $22.62 | $7.79 |

At 25x P/E — reasonable for a company still growing revenue at 20%+ with improving margins — the Base case implies $16.16. The Bull case at 20x P/E alone reaches $24.48, above the DCF Bull price of $23.20.

### Triangulation Summary

The three valuation approaches bracket the fair value range:

| Method | Bull | Base | Bear |
|--------|------|------|------|
| DCF (Gordon Growth) | $23.20 | $14.00 | $7.02 |
| EV/EBITDA exit at 10x | $20.61 | $11.95 | $5.38 |
| P/E exit at 25x | $30.59 | $16.16 | $5.56 |

The DCF and exit multiple approaches converge in the Base case around $12–16, with the DCF sitting near the midpoint. This convergence strengthens confidence in the valuation range. The key takeaway is that across methods, the Base case consistently implies a value materially above current trading levels, and even the Bear case at moderate multiples implies meaningful equity value.

## Key Risks

**Agent retention and competitive dynamics.** REAX's model can be replicated. If competitors match commission structures and equity incentives, agent growth could stall. The revenue share flywheel creates switching costs, but they are not insurmountable.

**Housing market cyclicality.** Transaction volumes are sensitive to mortgage rates, inventory, and consumer confidence. A prolonged downturn (as partially modeled in the Bear case) compresses every revenue line.

**SBC dilution.** While declining as a percentage of revenue, SBC remains significant ($68M in 2025). RSU vesting and option exercises added ~22K shares (10% dilution) in 2025. The $40M annual buyback partially offsets this, but net dilution continues in the near term.

**Financial services execution risk.** Title, Mortgage, and Wallet are nascent. Regulatory complexity (state-by-state licensing), integration challenges, and agent adoption friction could slow the attach rate trajectory. The Bull case FS assumptions are particularly ambitious.

**Terminal value concentration.** PV of Terminal Value is 76–79% of Enterprise Value across scenarios. The valuation is sensitive to long-term assumptions. However, the sensitivity table shows the price range remains positive even under conservative terminal assumptions.

**Concentration risk.** REAX operates exclusively in North American residential real estate. There is no geographic or product-line diversification beyond what the FS segments provide.

## Conclusion

REAX is a rare combination: a company growing revenue at 50%+ annually, generating positive free cash flow, operating debt-free, and trading at a discount to intrinsic value because GAAP metrics are temporarily distorted by non-cash SBC expense. The financial services opportunity — with 47–82% gross margins on products sold to a captive agent base — provides a credible pathway to meaningfully higher blended margins, even under conservative adoption assumptions.

The Base case implies $14.00/share. The Bear case, which assumes materially slower growth and limited FS adoption, still implies $7.02. The Bull case reaches $23.20. The range of outcomes is wide, but the risk/reward is favorable for a patient investor who understands the adjusted metrics and is willing to underwrite the FS option.

The right mental model is not "low-margin brokerage trading at a rich multiple" but rather "high-growth platform with an emerging high-margin financial services engine, temporarily obscured by non-cash GAAP charges, and valued as if the FS optionality has minimal worth."
